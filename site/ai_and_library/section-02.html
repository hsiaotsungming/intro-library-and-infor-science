Reader-Facing AI Applications

How can AI become part of reader services in libraries? This unit first explains the difference between traditional service chatbots and generative AI services. It then introduces two cases in Taiwan: the National Library of Public Information and National Taiwan University Library.

1. Changes Brought by Generative Interaction 2. Case: Xiao-Shu at NLPI 3. Case: AI Applications at NTU Library 4. Pros and Cons of Using AI in Reader Services 5. Summary References

1. Changes Brought by Generative Interaction

In the past, tools such as FAQs or user guides often depended on fixed question-and-answer databases. After a user entered a question, the system tried to match keywords and return the closest answer. If a reader's question was too conversational, too vague, or included several needs at once, the system might fail to understand it correctly. The same problem appears in catalog search interfaces, where users often need some prior understanding of the search system and its content before they can use it smoothly. This creates a higher barrier for users who are not familiar with library systems.

Generative AI changes this interaction. It responds to user input by drawing on its own knowledge or techniques such as retrieval-augmented generation, or RAG. This allows users to ask questions in language that is much closer to everyday speech, such as asking for science books for junior high school students or asking where to study quietly on a first visit to the library. Users do not need to know the formal classification system, service names, or search fields in advance. They can describe their needs in their own words. This may lower the barrier for new readers, children, and older adults.

However, the strength of generative AI is also its risk. Because it can produce fluent text, wrong answers can still look very convincing. When libraries introduce AI services, they need to consider this risk and prepare responses to it.

2. Case: Xiao-Shu at the National Library of Public Information

Xiao-Shu at the National Library of Public Information is a representative case of using generative AI in reader services. Xiao-Shu was jointly designed and developed by the National Library of Public Information and the Software Engineering and Technology Center at Tunghai University, and it has been described as Taiwan's first AI smart librarian. Using ChatGPT as its generative AI core, Xiao-Shu combines the image of a real librarian with human voice synthesis technology to provide reader consultation, collection recommendations, and everyday chat interaction.

Xiao-Shu combines a language model, a virtual librarian persona, and voice synthesis technology. Readers can interact with it by voice, and the character-based interface simulates something closer to face-to-face service.

Readers can ask for suggestions by age, subject, author, or situation, and Xiao-Shu then provides suitable collection lists or popular reading lists. If readers provide a specific title, Xiao-Shu can also help check detailed holding information and locations, and guide readers to the relevant floor to find related topics. It can also answer questions about library rules and services, such as how to apply for a library card or reserve a discussion room.

Real-Time Consultation Entry Point

It helps answer common service questions and serves as a first-line service contact point.

Collection Guide

It turns reader needs into book recommendations and collection location information.

Service Interface

Through a virtual librarian and voice interaction, it reduces the sense of distance readers may feel when facing a system.

3. Case: AI Applications at NTU Library

AI in libraries is not limited to general question answering. It can also support academic research. On the NTU Library website, readers can find the service interface of the AI librarian Libraly, which greets users in both Chinese and English and offers service support. NTU Library has also added an AI Research Assistant to its cloud library automation system, SLIM. Based on a user's question, the AI Research Assistant can provide five related references, an overview summary, and related research questions, helping users search for literature more efficiently and grasp topic information more quickly.

Comparing the two cases at NLPI and NTU Library shows an important difference. Xiao-Shu is more oriented toward reader consultation, collection recommendations, and spatial guidance in a public library. NTU Library's AI Research Assistant, by contrast, is more oriented toward research support in a university library. For undergraduate students, graduate students, and faculty members, the question is often not simply whether the library has a particular book, but which important references exist on a topic, how to begin searching for information, and what related research questions might be asked.

AI as a Research Starting Point

The AI Research Assistant places generative AI in the context of literature exploration and the first stage of research. It can help readers form an initial direction quickly, but users still need to return to original sources, databases, and librarian advice for checking, filtering, and citation.

It is worth noting that NTU Library specifically reminds users that the quality of AI-generated content may vary, and that users should verify it and cite it correctly. This is especially important because research support services require more accuracy and traceability than ordinary library service questions do.

4. Pros and Cons of Using AI in Reader Services

AI allows readers to ask questions in natural language, receive immediate responses to common questions, and get help with collection recommendations and research ideas. For readers who are not familiar with library systems, this service style is usually more approachable than a traditional search interface. But AI may also generate wrong information, invented data, or outdated rules as if they were true. In research support contexts, if users directly trust the documents, summaries, or citations suggested by AI, they may misunderstand the topic or even affect the accuracy of their research results.

At the same time, when libraries introduce AI services, readers also need to learn how to distinguish sources, verify citations, compare database records, and understand that generated content may include errors, bias, or hallucinations. Including prompt skills for generative AI, citation practices, and academic ethics in reader education reflects this direction. Overall, AI is well suited to serve as a first entry point for frequent, standardized, and lower-risk questions. Librarians, by contrast, remain responsible for services that involve complexity, professional judgment, and ethical responsibility.

Pros

  • Immediate responses to common questions
  • Lower barriers through natural language interaction
  • Collection recommendations based on interests and needs
  • Support for the first stage of research and topic exploration

Cons

  • Wrong information, invented data, or outdated rules
  • Convincing language even when the answer is wrong
  • Not suitable for high-risk questions involving data, ethics, or complaints
  • Need for ongoing maintenance as rules, collections, and systems change

5. Summary

From Xiao-Shu at the National Library of Public Information to NTU Library's AI librarian and AI Research Assistant, we can see that AI has already begun to become part of library service.

It can answer common questions, recommend collections, guide readers in using services, and help researchers quickly grasp a topic and an initial literature direction.

The value of AI does not lie in fully replacing librarians, but in redistributing service work so that it can serve as a first-layer entry point and initial helper.

For complex or ambiguous questions that require judgment, professional librarians remain indispensable, especially in research support and information literacy education.
